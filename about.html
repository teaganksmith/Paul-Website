About

A working biography, an outline of my research interests, and a record of the education, teaching, and fellowships that have shaped the work.

Biography

I came to the history and philosophy of science by way of a long career in neurobiology. My doctoral work at the University of Chicago, under Paul Grobstein, examined how the forebrain of the frog mediates visually guided orienting behavior. From there I held research positions at the University of Texas at Austin, the University of Arizona, Mercer University, the Beckman Institute at Illinois, and Bowling Green State University, working variously on hippocampal modelling, multisensory integration in the superior colliculus, and the use of the lateral line in Mexican blind cavefish.

In 2010 I returned to graduate school in the history and philosophy of science at Indiana University. My master's thesis, Ecological realism, prediction, and a new understanding of perception, attempted a synthesis of J. J. Gibson's ecological psychology with the predictive-processing framework now prominent in computational neuroscience. I am now completing my Ph.D. at the Institute for the History and Philosophy of Science at the University of Toronto.

Research Interests

My work clusters around three connected questions. What is perception? — and in particular, can the qualities we perceive be understood as real, perspectival features of the world rather than as additions made by the mind. How does science change? — the question at the heart of scientonomy, a research program that seeks an empirical theory of the dynamics of scientific belief. And what kinds of things are the agents and tools of science? — a social ontology of scientific practice, attentive to the cognitive instruments and institutions that make inquiry possible.

Teaching

At the University of Toronto I have served as a teaching assistant for courses in the history and philosophy of science (HPS100, HPS120, HPS250) and in the study of creativity (CRE345). For several years I have supervised undergraduate research through Victoria College's Scientonomy Research Program and the Scholars-in-Residence program, co-authoring papers and book chapters with students.

Earlier teaching includes a visiting assistant professorship at Williams College, where I developed and taught a course in neuroethology with laboratory work on normal and brain-lesioned frogs, and co-instructorships in neuroethology and sensory ecology at Bowling Green State University. I have taught introductory biology, cellular and molecular biology, cognitive neuroscience, animal behavior, and a self-developed course in mathematics for cognitive science.

Research Experience

Selected appointments include: research scientist at the Beckman Institute, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign (1998–2004), working with Thomas Anastasio on computational models of multisensory integration; research assistant professor at Bowling Green State University (2005–2009), with Sheryl Coombs on lateral-line mediated navigation in the Mexican blind cavefish; and research associate at the University of Arizona (1992–1995), with Bruce McNaughton on computational models of the rodent hippocampal formation. I currently serve as co-editor in chief of the Encyclopedia of Scientonomy.
